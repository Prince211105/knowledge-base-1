# WBS applicability across all project types

## Overview

Explains why the WBS is relevant to every project type.

## Detailed Summary

The common thread across all projects is that every project produces deliverables. Therefore, the WBS is applicable to and required for every project because all projects implement changes to provide new or different outcomes for customers, whether internal or external. These outcomes may be delivered as a product, service, or process, and tangible includes things the customer can see, touch, feel, or experience.

## Content

Because every project creates some form of output, every project can and should use a WBS. The specific nature of the output may vary: a physical product, a service experience, or a repeatable process. Regardless of type, the WBS documents the components of that outcome. This makes the WBS the common scope-defining thread across product, service, and process creation projects.

## Best Practices

- Tailor the deliverable hierarchy to the type of outcome being produced.
- Remember that experiential service components can still be represented as deliverables.

## Examples

- A bicycle project, a wedding planning service project, and an office relocation process project can all be represented with WBS deliverables.

## Warnings

- Assuming only physical products need a WBS can leave service or process projects underdefined.

## Limitations

- Different project types may require different decomposition styles even though the WBS concept remains applicable.

## Troubleshooting

- If stakeholders believe their project has no deliverables, ask what outcome the customer will receive or experience.

## Decision Rules

- If a project produces any customer outcome, represent that outcome through deliverables in a WBS.
