# Hierarchical structure of the WBS

## Overview

Explains how the WBS is organized in parent and child layers of granularity.

## Detailed Summary

The WBS is a hierarchical document built in layers of granularity. Each successive child layer clarifies the deliverables above it at the parent level. Stakeholders start with major top-level deliverable components and then decompose each one into more detailed components. This hierarchical viewpoint helps stakeholders visualize the project solution and align on what must be provided by the project.

## Content

A WBS is organized as a hierarchy. The top level shows major deliverable groupings. Each lower level provides more detail about the deliverable above it. Parent deliverables are clarified by child deliverables, and decomposition continues until the team has enough detail to understand what will be produced. The hierarchical view is useful because it structures large or complex scope into logical relationships of related outputs and helps stakeholders see the full solution before design and build effort begins.

## Best Practices

- Begin with major deliverable categories, then drill down only as needed.
- Use the hierarchy to clarify rather than to overcomplicate scope.

## Examples

- At a top level, a training course WBS may include Training Materials and Logistics; lower levels then break these into more specific deliverables.

## Warnings

- A flat list of items loses the relational clarity provided by a hierarchical WBS.

## Limitations

- Hierarchy alone does not guarantee completeness; the team must still validate scope coverage.

## Troubleshooting

- If stakeholders cannot see how deliverables relate, reorganize the WBS into clearer parent-child groupings.

## Decision Rules

- Create a child level only when it meaningfully clarifies the parent deliverable.
