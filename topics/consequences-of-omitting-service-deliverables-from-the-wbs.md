# Consequences of omitting service deliverables from the WBS

## Overview

Describes how omitted deliverables in a service WBS can cause late planning changes.

## Detailed Summary

If a service-related deliverable such as Minister is left off the wedding planning WBS, the activities for engaging that deliverable may also be left out of planning and budgeting until the last minute. This kind of oversight likely drives schedule and cost change requests.

## Content

Service WBS omissions can have direct downstream effects. When a needed deliverable is absent from the WBS, the corresponding activities, costs, and planning assumptions may also be absent from the schedule and budget. The result is often late discovery, urgent replanning, and formal change requests affecting schedule and cost.

## Can Answer

- How WBS omissions create downstream planning issues
- Why late changes happen when deliverables are omitted

## Best Practices

- Review service WBS branches for all customer-facing and enabling deliverables.
- Use omitted-item reviews before baselining schedule and budget.

## Examples

- Omitting the Minister from the WBS may lead to last-minute effort and budget changes to secure that service.

## Warnings

- Even a seemingly obvious omitted deliverable can cause formal schedule and cost changes.

## Limitations

- Not all late changes come from WBS omissions, but omissions are a common preventable cause.

## Troubleshooting

- If an unexpected service need emerges late, check whether it was absent from the WBS and related planning documents.

## Decision Rules

- If a deliverable would require planning, budgeting, or coordination, include it explicitly in the WBS to avoid late change requests.
