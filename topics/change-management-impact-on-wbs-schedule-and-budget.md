# Change management impact on WBS, schedule, and budget

## Overview

Explains how approved scope changes propagate through WBS-based planning artifacts.

## Detailed Summary

If change management requests are approved to increase or decrease project scope, the WBS deliverables are updated to reflect the approved changes by adding or removing deliverables as needed. Those WBS updates in turn add or remove activities and drive schedule and budget changes. The WBS therefore supports objective management of scope changes and downstream plan updates.

## Content

The WBS is central to managing approved scope change. When project scope expands or contracts, the WBS should be revised first to reflect the new set of deliverables. Once the deliverable structure changes, the related activities, schedule, and budget can be adjusted accordingly. This provides a clear traceable path from scope change to planning impact and helps prevent arbitrary changes to time or cost without a corresponding scope basis.

## Can Answer

- How change requests affect planning artifacts
- Why the WBS is central to change control
- How scope additions or removals propagate downstream

## Best Practices

- Update the WBS as soon as scope changes are approved.
- Trace every schedule or budget change back to a corresponding WBS deliverable change.

## Examples

- Adding a new deliverable to the WBS creates new activities and likely increases schedule duration and budget.
- Removing an optional service deliverable removes related activities and costs.

## Warnings

- Changing schedule or budget without updating the WBS can break traceability to scope.

## Limitations

- Only approved changes should alter the WBS baseline.

## Troubleshooting

- If schedule or cost has changed without clear reason, inspect whether the WBS was altered or should have been altered.

## Decision Rules

- When approved scope changes occur, first update WBS deliverables, then update activities, schedule, and budget.
