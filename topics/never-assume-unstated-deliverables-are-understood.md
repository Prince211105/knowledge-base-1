# Never assume unstated deliverables are understood

## Overview

Explains why all expected deliverables must be explicitly represented in the WBS.

## Detailed Summary

A recommended WBS guideline is to never assume stakeholders will naturally know a deliverable element is part of the project if it is not included in the WBS. If a deliverable is omitted from the WBS, it may be omitted from subsequent planning and may be overlooked until late in the project.

## Content

The WBS should explicitly list deliverables rather than relying on stakeholder assumptions or implied understanding. Unstated items are vulnerable to being forgotten during planning, scheduling, and budgeting. Explicit representation is especially important for items that may seem obvious to one stakeholder but not to others. The WBS serves as the visible agreement on scope, so anything expected should be shown.

## Best Practices

- List all in-scope deliverables explicitly.
- Review the WBS for items stakeholders believe are 'understood' but not shown.

## Examples

- If the Minister deliverable is left off a wedding service WBS, planning for engaging the minister may be missed until late.

## Warnings

- Omitted deliverables can cause late schedule and budget changes.

## Limitations

- Explicit listing does not replace later activity definition, but it prevents scope invisibility.

## Troubleshooting

- If a forgotten item emerges late, check whether it was omitted from the WBS and update related plans accordingly.

## Decision Rules

- If a deliverable matters to project scope, include it explicitly in the WBS even if it seems obvious.
