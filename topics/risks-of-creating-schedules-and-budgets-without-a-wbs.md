# Risks of creating schedules and budgets without a WBS

## Overview

Explains why planning without first creating a WBS increases risk.

## Detailed Summary

When teams create a project schedule or budget without first creating a WBS, they increase the risk of producing unsubstantiated and inaccurate schedules and budgets. Without the WBS, they lack sufficient information about the product solution deliverables that will meet project objectives. The safest way to substantiate early schedules and budgets is to base them on stakeholder knowledge of the deliverables that will be produced.

## Content

Skipping the WBS in planning creates a risk that schedules and budgets will be incomplete, unsupported, or inaccurate. This happens because the team has not yet established a complete picture of the deliverables required to meet project objectives. Even at project initiation, when detailed planning is not yet possible, a high-level WBS can provide enough clarity to support a rough order of magnitude schedule and budget. The WBS gives the team a defensible basis for what is included in planning estimates.

## Best Practices

- Produce at least a high-level WBS before drafting initial schedules and budgets.
- Use deliverables as the basis for estimate scope boundaries.

## Examples

- A team estimates effort before identifying all deliverables and later discovers missing work, causing schedule and cost increases.

## Warnings

- Schedules and budgets created without a WBS may omit required deliverables.

## Limitations

- A high-level WBS supports only high-level estimates, not final detailed commitments.

## Troubleshooting

- If estimates keep changing unexpectedly, check whether previously unidentified deliverables are being discovered after planning began.

## Decision Rules

- If deliverables are not yet understood, treat schedule and budget figures as rough order of magnitude estimates only.
